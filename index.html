Ideals of Democracy Locke vs. Hobbes Federalists vs. Anti-Federalists Key Court Cases

Ideals of Democracy

  • Popular Sovereignty: The government derives its authority from the consent of the governed.
  • Judicial Review: Allows courts to determine the constitutionality of laws (*Marbury v. Madison*).
  • Limited Government: Government powers are restricted by the Constitution.
  • Federalism: Power is divided between national and state governments.

Popular Sovereignty

The government's power comes from the people.

Judicial Review

Courts decide if laws align with the Constitution.

Locke vs. Hobbes

  • Locke: Advocated for natural rights (life, liberty, property) and a government that protects those rights.
  • Hobbes: Believed humans are naturally selfish and need a strong central authority to maintain order.

Federalists vs. Anti-Federalists

  • Federalists: Supported a strong central government; argued for the Constitution.
  • Anti-Federalists: Opposed a strong central government; demanded a Bill of Rights to protect individual liberties.

Key Court Cases

  • McCulloch v. Maryland: Upheld federal supremacy over states and validated implied powers.
  • US v. Lopez: Limited Congress's use of the Commerce Clause.
  • Baker v. Carr: Established "one person, one vote" in redistricting.
  • Shaw v. Reno: Declared racial gerrymandering unconstitutional.
  • Citizens United v. FEC: Allowed unlimited political spending by corporations and unions.
